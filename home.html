James Robert Oppenheimer

Physicist, intellectual, and controversial figure in nuclear history. He led the Manhattan Project and later became a voice of moral reflection in the atomic age.

About

Julius Robert Oppenheimer (1904–1967) was an American theoretical physicist best known for his role as the director of the Los Alamos Laboratory during World War II. Often called the "father of the atomic bomb", Oppenheimer later advocated for international control of nuclear power and opposed the hydrogen bomb development.

Journey

Early Years

Born in New York City to a wealthy Jewish family, Oppenheimer showed brilliance early and studied at Harvard, Cambridge, and Göttingen.

Manhattan Project

As director of Los Alamos Laboratory, he oversaw the creation of the first nuclear weapons, culminating in the Trinity test in 1945.

Postwar Life

He served as a chief advisor to the U.S. Atomic Energy Commission, but his opposition to the hydrogen bomb led to a controversial security hearing in 1954.
